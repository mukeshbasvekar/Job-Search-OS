# Offer Negotiation Playbook

A protocol for the moment the search has been building toward. Negotiation is the highest-leverage 30 minutes in the entire process, and the easiest to get wrong by rushing it.

---

## Before You Respond to Any Offer

**Get it in writing first.** A verbal offer is not an offer. Recruiters and hiring managers move fast on the phone and slower on paper, on purpose. Before you react to anything, say: "This sounds great, can you send the written offer so I can review the full details?" Negotiate against the document, not the phone call.

**Get the full comp breakdown, not just the headline number.** A verbal "$180K" can hide a lot. Ask for, in writing:
- Base salary
- Bonus or commission structure: is it a target or a guarantee? What's the quota basis? Are there accelerators above 100%? Is there a ramp period, and is the ramp quota lower?
- Equity: number of shares/options, strike price, vesting schedule, cliff length, and the company's most recent valuation (so you can do real math on what the grant is worth, not just the share count)
- Signing bonus: is it a true bonus, or a forgivable loan that claws back if you leave early?
- Start date and any conditions attached to it (visa filings, background checks, references)

**Identify what's actually negotiable before you ask for anything.** Base salary, signing bonus, equity refresh timing, start date, and title are usually negotiable. Quota structure and standard benefits usually are not. Don't spend your one ask on something fixed.

---

## The Negotiation Conversation

**Don't give a number first if you can avoid it.** If asked for your expectation early in the process, anchor with a range tied to market data, not a single number: "Based on what I'm seeing for this level and scope, I'd expect something in the $X-Y range, but I'm flexible depending on the full picture."

**If you have competing interest, use it transparently, not as a bluff.** "I'm further along with another company and want to be upfront that timing matters to me" is honest leverage. Threatening a competing offer you don't actually have is a trust risk you can't recover from if it's tested.

**Make one ask, not a list.** A negotiation with five asks reads as someone testing limits. A negotiation with one clear, well-reasoned ask reads as someone who knows what they need. If you have multiple priorities, rank them and lead with the one that matters most.

**Tie the ask to something defensible, not just "I want more."** "Given the scope includes [X], and comparable roles at this stage are tracking [Y], I'd like to discuss the base" lands better than an unanchored number.

**Expect a counter, and decide your floor before you're in the room.** Know in advance what number or set of terms you'd actually walk away from. Negotiating without a floor means you'll talk yourself into accepting whatever's offered in the moment.

---

## Special Case: Visa-Dependent Offers

If your ability to start is contingent on a visa transfer, petition filing, or any immigration process, get the following in writing before accepting:

- Whether the start date is tied to petition filing or to approval/receipt confirmation. These are very different risk profiles, treat them differently.
- Who bears the risk if the timeline slips: does your offer remain valid if the process takes longer than expected?
- Whether any signing bonus or other term is contingent on a specific start date, and what happens if that date moves for reasons outside your control.

Get this in writing even if the recruiter says it "shouldn't be an issue." Verbal reassurance does not survive a process delay. A written term does.

---

## After You Have the Final Terms

**Ask for 24-48 hours before responding**, even if you're inclined to accept. A pause signals you're taking the decision seriously, and it gives you room to sanity-check the math without pressure.

**Read the written offer letter line by line before signing**, not just the summary email. Verbal negotiation points sometimes don't make it into the final document. If something discussed verbally is missing from the letter, ask for it to be added before you sign, not after.

**Don't negotiate again after accepting.** Renegotiating post-acceptance, outside of a real change in circumstances, burns trust with a team you're about to join. Get it right before you sign.

---

## What Not to Do

- Don't negotiate over text message. Push everything substantive to email or a call, so there's a record.
- Don't let urgency from the other side compress your decision window unfairly. "We need an answer today" is sometimes real and sometimes a tactic. Ask directly: "Is today a hard deadline, or is there flexibility if I need until tomorrow?"
- Don't accept a verbal "we'll figure out the details after you join" on anything that materially affects your compensation or your ability to actually start the job.
- Don't apologize for negotiating. A reasonable, well-researched ask is a normal part of the process, not an imposition.
