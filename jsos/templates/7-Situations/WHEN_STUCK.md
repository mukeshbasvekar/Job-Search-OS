# When You're Stuck — Situational Playbook

A diagnostic guide for every common stuck point in a job search. Find your situation, follow the protocol. Don't change what isn't broken.

---

## How to Diagnose Your Situation

Before doing anything, answer these three questions:

1. **Are emails going out?** (Track 1 running at volume?)
2. **Are replies coming in?** (Reply rate above 30%?)
3. **Are calls being booked?** (2+ HM/founder calls per week?)

That tells you exactly where the break is.

```
Emails going out? → No  →  Situation G: Momentum collapsed
                  → Yes ↓
Reply rate ≥ 30%? → No  →  Situation A: Email isn't working
                  → Yes ↓
2+ calls/week?   → No  →  Situation B: Replies but no meetings
                  → Yes ↓
Converting?       → No  →  Situation C or D: Interview problem
                  → Yes →  System is working. Keep running it.
```

---

## Situation A — No Replies to Cold Emails

**What it looks like:** Sending consistently, reply rate below 30% after 50+ sends.

**What it usually means:** The email itself. Not the channel. Cold email to founders is the highest-leverage channel in a job search — if it's not working, the problem is the email, not the approach.

**What to change — one variable at a time, in this order:**

1. **Subject line first.** Most emails die here. Test one subject line variation against your current one for 20 sends. Something specific beats something clever.
2. **Personalization line second.** The opening sentence that shows you actually looked at their company. If it's generic, the rest of the email doesn't matter.
3. **Bullets third.** Are you leading with what you've done or with what you can do for them? Lead with their problem, not your resume.

**What not to change:**
- Don't switch channels (LinkedIn DMs, job boards) because email isn't converting. Fix the email.
- Don't send more volume as a substitute for fixing the quality.
- Don't add more context or length. Shorter almost always performs better.

**Check also:**
- Are you reaching founders/CEOs directly or are emails going to HR/recruiting?
- Are you emailing companies that are genuinely interesting to you? Personalization is detectable — if you don't care, it shows.
- Has it been fewer than 2 weeks at this stage? Give the system time before declaring it broken.

**Trigger:** Fix when reply rate < 30% **after 50+ sends**. Don't optimize before you have signal.

## Situation B — Getting Replies but No Meetings Booked

**What it looks like:** Good reply rate (30%+), but replies aren't converting to scheduled calls. HM+ rate below 40%.

**What it usually means:** One of three things:
1. You're reaching the wrong person (recruiting/HR instead of hiring manager or founder)
2. The email is creating the wrong expectation (they're replying to redirect you, not to meet)
3. Follow-up isn't happening fast enough (replies go stale quickly)

**What to do:**

- **Check who is replying.** If replies are from HR or a recruiter rather than the founder or hiring manager, adjust your targeting. For early-stage companies (sub-50 people), email the founder directly.
- **Respond same day.** A reply that sits for 24+ hours loses momentum. Treat every reply as a meeting that hasn't been booked yet.
- **Make the ask explicit.** "Would you be open to a 15-minute call this week?" is clearer than "I'd love to connect." Low friction, specific.
- **Check the email's promise.** If the bullets oversell what you can do, the reply will be skeptical, not excited. Ground your bullets in specifics.

**What not to do:**
- Don't interpret a "not a fit right now" reply as system failure. That's a correct filter working.
- Don't follow up more than once after a no-reply. Two emails (initial + one follow-up) is the standard.

## Situation C — Getting Meetings but Not Moving Past First Round

**What it looks like:** First calls are happening, but second rounds aren't. The call feels fine but nothing follows.

**What it usually means:** The narrative isn't landing. Either the story doesn't hang together, the interviewer isn't clear on why you fit their specific problem, or you're not asking the questions that show genuine interest.

**What to fix:**

**Your narrative arc.** Can you explain your path in 90 seconds in a way that makes the next logical step obvious? If the interviewer has to work to understand why you're a fit, you've lost. The arc should do that work for them.

**Specificity over length.** One concrete proof point (shipped X, grew Y to Z users, saved $X in manual work) lands harder than three vague claims.

**The "why this company" answer.** This is almost always weak and always matters. It needs one real, specific thing about what they're building — not generic startup enthusiasm. Generic = forgettable.

**The close.** At the end of every first call: "What would make someone ideal for this role, and is there anything you'd want to know more about?" This surfaces objections before they become silent rejections.

**What to audit:**
- Record yourself answering the 5 most common first-round questions. Listen back. Is the story clear? Does it build?
- Are you talking about what you did or what you're going to do? First rounds are won on what you've done.

## Situation D — Stuck in Final Rounds, No Offer

**What it looks like:** Getting to late rounds consistently, but not closing. Multiple final rounds with no offers.

**What it usually means:** One of: (a) objections you're not handling, (b) a candidate with more direct experience winning, or (c) soft signals you're missing in the process.

**What to do:**

**Debrief every final round.** After each one, write down:
- What questions felt shaky?
- What did they seem most interested in?
- What objections came up (directly or indirectly)?
- Did you ask what the decision timeline was?

**Prepare for the objections you're not getting.** The most dangerous objections are the ones interviewers think but don't say. Common ones: years of experience relative to the role, transitions in your background, whether you'll stay. Prepare answers for these proactively — bring them up yourself before they do.

**The "what are your concerns" close.** Before ending a final round: "Is there anything about my background or experience that you'd want to understand better?" This gives you one more shot to address a silent disqualifier.

**Don't stop the pipeline.** The worst thing to do in late rounds is pause outreach waiting for a decision. Keep the system running. Leverage (other rounds in progress) also makes a difference in offer conversations.

## Situation E — Running Out of Companies to Contact

**What it looks like:** The scored shortlist is under 20 companies. You've been working from the same stage for 3+ weeks.

**What to do — in order:**

1. **Scrape a new funding stage.** If you've been on Series A, run Series B or Seed. Each stage gives you a fresh batch. (`jsos scrape --stage seed`)
2. **Lower the score threshold temporarily.** Score 5 companies you previously filtered out. Some will be worth a shot with the right framing.
3. **Add Track 2 intentionally.** Companies from LinkedIn, newsletters, Twitter/X, or conversations — these are often the highest-fit because your interest is genuine. Keep a running list as you encounter them.
4. **Start Track 3 if you haven't.** Big companies have slower cycles but consistent processes. They're lagging pipeline — if Track 1 closes fast, great. If not, Track 3 gives you something in the background.

**What not to do:**
- Don't stop sending while you rebuild the list. Send what you have while the new batch comes in.
- Don't lower quality standards just to hit volume. Sending 50 bad emails is worse than sending 20 good ones.

## Situation F — Getting Interviews for the Wrong Types of Roles

**What it looks like:** Getting into processes, but for roles that aren't what you're targeting. Wrong seniority, wrong function, wrong stage of company.

**What it usually means:** Your targeting or your positioning is off. Either you're emailing the wrong companies, or your email/resume is leading with the wrong narrative.

**Check your targeting first:**
- What role does your email pitch? Is it explicit?
- What does your suggested_role column in the shortlist say? Are you filtering by that?
- Are you applying to companies where your background is the obvious fit, or where you're a stretch?

**Check your positioning second:**
- What does the first bullet of your cold email say about what role you're going for?
- Does your resume lead with the experience most relevant to the roles you want?
- Are you over-indexing on titles you've held vs. the work you've done?

**The fix:**
- Be explicit in the email about what you're looking for. "I'd love to explore a founding PM or early PM role" is clearer than leaving it open-ended.
- Adjust the scoring weights in config.yaml to better reflect the role type and company stage you actually want.

## Situation G — Momentum Has Collapsed

**What it looks like:** The system has basically stopped. You haven't sent emails in days. The tracker is stale. You feel stuck.

**What it usually means:** Emotional fatigue, not strategic failure. Job searching is grinding — the system breaks when the person running it stops running it.

**The restart protocol:**

1. **Don't audit the strategy. Not yet.** When momentum collapses, the instinct is to conclude the strategy is wrong and start over. That's rarely true. The strategy is usually fine. The execution stopped.
2. **One small win first.** Send 5 emails before you do anything else. Not 25. Five. Start the loop.
3. **Check the morning ritual.** Is there a consistent starting point to the day? Anchor moment → music → pick companies → send. The ritual is a forcing function. Without it, Track 1 slips every time.
4. **Come back to why.** Not the job title. The feeling of having landed — what does it actually unlock? Reconnect to that before opening the tracker.
5. **Then audit the metrics.** Once you've sent 5 emails, look at where things stand. Reply rate, calls booked this week, active rounds. That's your actual state, not your emotional read of it.

**What not to do:**
- Don't redesign the system when you haven't been executing it.
- Don't confuse "feeling stuck" with "the system isn't working." They're different diagnoses.
- Don't add new tactics. Adding complexity to a stopped system doesn't restart it.

## Situation H — Reply Rate Suddenly Dropped

**What it looks like:** You were getting good replies, and then something changed. Reply rate dropped from 40%+ to under 20%.

**What usually happened:**
- The subject line or email template is stale (similar to others flooding inboxes in your space)
- You've moved to a different cohort of companies where your positioning is less precise
- The personalization line has gotten more generic over time

**What to do:**
- Pull the last 20 emails you sent. Read the personalization line on each one. Does each one feel like you actually looked at the company, or does it feel templated?
- Change the subject line first. Test a completely different framing for 10 sends.
- Check: are you still reaching founders/decision-makers, or has targeting drifted?

**The rule:** Change one variable at a time. Wait for 20-30 sends before evaluating the change. Changing everything at once means you learn nothing.

## What Not to Do in Any Situation

These are the most common mistakes that slow searches down. From the data, not guesswork:

- **Optimizing the email instead of sending it.** A B-grade email sent to 500 people beats an A-grade email sent to 50. Get it good enough and ship it.
- **Pausing the system when interviews start.** Keep Track 1 running even when you have active rounds. Pipeline dries up if you stop. The time between "sent" and "decision" is when you should be filling the next batch.
- **Tracking activity instead of outcomes.** Emails sent is not the goal. HM calls booked per week is the goal. If you're not hitting 2+/week, audit backward from there — don't just send more.
- **Changing strategy after one bad week.** One bad week is noise. Two bad weeks with consistent execution is signal.
- **Adding new channels instead of fixing existing ones.** Cold email is the highest-leverage channel. Fix it before you try something else.
- **Treating ATS applications as the primary pipeline.** They're a background habit, not the main driver. Don't let them expand.

---

## The One Metric That Matters

**2+ HM/founder calls booked per week.**

Everything else is a leading indicator. If you're hitting this number, the system is working — don't touch it. If you're not, trace backward through the funnel to find the break.

That's the whole diagnostic.
